The list of clusters and distances on the previous tab falls short of a presentation that is easy to interpret, particularly if there are hundreds of samples instead of our small set of six samples. A more effective representation of a cluster analysis is the figure on the right, which is called a dendrogram.

The bottom of the dendrogram is defined by the individual samples—in this case using the sample numbers 1 through 6—ordered in a way that locates similar samples near each other. The individual samples, also known as leaves, generally are equally spaced from each other. Rising upwards from each leaf is a vertical line—called a branch—that extends to a height (distance) equal to its first appearance in a cluster; thus the branches that begin with samples 3 and 4 end at a height of 0.26, the distance between samples 3 and 4. A horizontal line connects these samples, identifying the first cluster. The cluster of samples 2 and 6 is defined in the same way with a height of 0.36. Vertical lines extend from the horizontal lines for these two clusters, each rising to a height of 1.04 and linking together the first two clusters.
