For the first step in a cluster analysis, each sample is its own entity and calculating the similarity matrix is straight-forward. Once we form a cluster, however, its distance from other samples and clusters becomes unclear.

The four figures on the upper right illustrates the problem. Each figure shows an identical set of two clusters, one with three samples (in blue) and one with two samples (in green). The distance between these clusters is defined in terms of the individual samples that make up each cluster, a process called agglomeration. There are many agglomeration methods, three of which are shown here: single, where similarity is the smallest distance between the samples in the two clusters; complete, where similarity is the greatest distance between the samples in the two clusters; average, where similarity is the average of all possible distances between the samples in the two clusters; and centroid, where similarity is the distance between the centroid—the small, black circle—for each cluster.

Two sets of radio buttons allow you to define how distance is calculated and how clusters are agglomerated. Below the radio buttons is a list that shows the progress of the cluster analysis, The samples that comprise a cluster appear within curly braces; thus {3,4} is a cluster of samples 3 and 4, and {2,3,4,6} is a cluster of samples 2, 3, 4, and 6. For each step the distance between the clusters is given.
