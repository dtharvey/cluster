The table on the upper right shows values of two measurements (\(a\) and \(b\)) for each of six samples (1–6); this is the same data from the introduction. The first step in a cluster analysis is to determine the distance between each pair of samples as a measure of similarity. Two common measures of distance are the Euclidean distance and the Manhattan distance \[\text{Euclidean: }d(P,Q) = \sqrt{(a_2 - a_1)^2 + (b_2 - b_1)^2} \quad \text{Manhattan: }d(P,Q) = \sqrt{|a_2 - a_1| + |b_2 - b_1|}\] where \(d(P,Q)\) is the distance between samples \(P\) and \(Q\), and \(a_1\), \(a_2\), \(b_1\), and \(b_2\) are their measured values. For example, the Euclidean distance between the third and the sixth samples is \[d(P,Q) = \sqrt{(-1.90 - (-0.90))^2 + (-(-0.17) -(-0.12))^2} = 1.04\]
